# Benign vs Dangerous Regeneration

Version: v2.3

This document separates benign regeneration from dangerous regeneration.

## Core distinction

Regeneration alone is not failure.

A trajectory may regenerate debt many times and still remain non-obstructive if compensation repeatedly erases obstruction potential.

Therefore:

regeneration != obstruction

## Benign regeneration

Benign regeneration occurs when:

1. Debt reappears.
2. Compensation follows.
3. Surplus remains positive.
4. Shadow is erased or does not preserve obstruction.
5. No self-preserving debt line forms.

Benign regeneration supports the native closure direction.

## Dangerous regeneration

Dangerous regeneration occurs when:

1. Debt reappears after compensation.
2. Shadow persists.
3. Later debt remains dependent on earlier debt.
4. Compensation becomes weaker, delayed, or insufficient.
5. Obstruction potential is not erased.

Dangerous regeneration identifies where closure must be tested harder.

## Obstruction threshold

Dangerous regeneration becomes an obstruction candidate only when it satisfies:

persistent debt + non-erased shadow + insufficient compensation + no closure event

Without all of these, it remains stress, not obstruction.

## Relation to previous evidence

The adversarial compensation scan found many regenerated bad-window structures in selected finite candidates.
All were compensated in that scan.

This supports benign or compensated regeneration for those selected finite cases.
It does not prove global closure.

## Rule

Benign regeneration is evidence for closure.
Dangerous regeneration is evidence for further testing.
Obstruction-preserving regeneration is the native counterexample target.
